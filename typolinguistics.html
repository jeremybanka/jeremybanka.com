Typolinguistics

The following is the first in a series of brief glossaries relating linguistics and typographic style. This entry focuses on writing systems, text, and differentiating rendered form from abstraction.

TEXT Text is a visuotactile recording of linguistic information. There are three methods to creating, or rendering, text: writing is produced by marking a surface once per stroke; lettering is drawn text, in which strokes are built from multiple marks; typography is printed text, produced by a mechanical system, in which multiple strokes are typically produced at once. What you are currently reading is a typographic text printed on your computer screen in my typeface, Talhertz.

LANGUAGE Language (uncountable) is a shorthand I will use for the linguistic information that text records. Language lives in the mind – it has no visuotactile formal qualities of its own. These belong to the ducti of the Character, hand, typeface, or font in which a text is rendered.

SCRIPT A script is a visuotactile system for recording linguistic information. A script is made of graphs. People use scripts to interpret language as text. Multiple languages may use the same script, or versions of the same script. For example, Russian and Ukrainian both use versions of the Cyrillic script, while English and Italian use versions of the Latin script.

Logographic System A script whose graphs represent morphemes, or units of meaning.

Syllabary A script whose graphs represent syllables or morae (units of speech comprising one or multiple phonemes, but interpreted as a single sound).

Abugida A script whose graphs represent consonant-vowel pairs.

Abjad A script whose graphs represent consonantal phonemes, in which vowel content must be inferred through context.

Alphabet A script whose graphs represent vowel and consonant phonemes.

Featural Alphabet A script whose graphs represent phonetic features.

STROKE A mark formed by a writing implement.

CHARACTER(This word is a little tricky.)

uncapitalized also glyph: A rendered (written, drawn, or printed) unit of linguistic information: an element of a [text].

LetterAlso Phonogram: A character that represents a vowel or consonant phoneme: a member of an alphabet.

GraphThe abstracted, fundamental shape of a character. That is, the character in your mind’s eye, not rendered in text.

GraphemeA grapheme is the smallest contrastive unit in a script, made of one or multiple strokes. A graph may comprise one or multiple graphemes. For example, á (aacute) contains the a and the ´ (acute) graphemes.

Ductus(As distinct from graph) a rendered character’s formal qualities.

capitalized A commonly recognized visual style in which the characters of a script are rendered. A brief passage in the italic Character.
