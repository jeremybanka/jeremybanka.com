MY STORY

In my sophomore year of high school, I took an English class that changed my life. In stunning contrast to my previous English teachers, Mr. Hammonds was a grammar stickler. Observing correctly that proper conventions were utterly neglected in our school’s curricula, he would give us a grammatical warmup of his own design each day of his class. I quickly discovered that I intuitively used almost every rule Mr. Hammonds described, but had never heard them referred to before.

An obsession began. For the next year or so, I pored over Wikipedia pages on linguistic typology and ultimately synthesized my own material from what I learned. By my senior year, I had developed an aprioristic constructed language called Srailese with a lexicon of around a thousand English-word equivalents and a slew of bizarre linguistic features—for instance, a unified word class that could be dually inflected as verbs or nouns, and a set of five noun genders/classes based on East Asian classical elements.

It was during my period of work on Srailese that I read my mother’s copy of Ellen Lupton’s Thinking with Type. I fell in love with Martin Majoor’s Scala, in which the volume was set. I based the first typographic alphabet for Srailese on Majoor’s digital–Baroque masterpiece, and the second on Akzidenz Grotesk.

I discovered that the visual aspects of language gripped me even more strongly than the structural aspects, and during my first year of college, embarked upon typographic design as a personal hobby. With the extracurricular guidance of a typographically knowledgeable professor, I proceeded to design the 2015 Ergata type family, my first set of Roman fonts.

Since my freshman year, I have continued to design typography, chiefly text faces for my own design work. In 2017, I published Talhertz, my first public-release font.
